pricefixed · open tools · rent transparency · nyc first

The rent number on your lease was not set by a person. It was set by software.

Landlords across the country feed their vacancies into shared pricing algorithms, and those algorithms raise rents in lockstep across competitors. The Department of Justice sued over it and called it what it is: price-fixing.

You cannot audit an algorithm without the data it feeds on, and almost none of that data is public. So this starts where every real answer starts: pulling the data into the open, owned by no one, for anyone to build on.

the wall

The data is the wall.

Zillow and StreetEasy are paid by landlords and brokers, so the real prices and the real history stay locked behind them. But there is a crack in the wall: landlords have to publish their availability to lease their own units. Those landlord-direct feeds are public by necessity.

pricefixed reads them, snapshots every price and lease term on every pull, and writes it all to a plain SQLite file. No account, no key, no lock-in. The portals show you today's ask and throw the rest away. Run this on a cron and you keep what they discard: the real trajectory of what every unit asked, over time.

what ships today

Three primitives, one SQLite file.

the feeds

Scrapers that stay simple

10 reference adapters for NYC landlord-direct sources, each about 30 lines, Python standard library only. FEEDS.md maps 36 more, verified and ready to build.

the history

Snapshots the sites discard

Every pull writes one row per listing to price_history. Price drops, lease-term changes, delistings: recoverable forever, sellable by no one.

the record

One record per building

A standardized public record composed from city data: who owns it, which portfolio it belongs to, whether it is rent stabilized. 3,197 buildings so far.

Straight about what this is today: the data layer, not the detector. The history is 14 pulls young and compounds from here. Exposing the pricing algorithms is the destination, and you do not get there without this floor.

where this goes

Three steps, in order.

01

The feedsships today

Maintained, dependency-free scrapers for landlord-direct sources. NYC first, because it is the hardest and the most walled.

02

The inventory

A published, open dataset of what the feeds return over time, so nobody has to run the scrapers to get the history.

03

The algorithms

Reverse-engineering how landlord pricing software actually sets your rent, in public, so the thing setting the price can finally be seen.

On scraping: it has a bad name, and it is also how every big real-estate company got its data in the first place. They just block you when you try to get it back. These tools read what landlords publish to lease their own units, at gentle rates, from public pages.
